General Objective: Learn how geographic data gets distorted depending on the projections and recognize common coordinates systems and projections used in the United States.

Specific objectives / activities:

  1. Modeling the earth as a sphere, an ellipsoid or geoid
  2. Reviewing the geographic coordinate system GCS
  3. World map projections
  4. Other Projections and how to reproject your data

1. Modeling the earth as a sphere, an ellipsoid or geoid

Required reading:
Read this document that explains the use of spheres, ellipsoid and geoids to model the shape of Earth
Read this short description of a geoid by the USGS. Make sure you understand this figure from the USGS
Read this short description of a geoid by NOAA
Read this NY Times article about the measuring the vertical datum
By the end of this section you should be able to:
- Define a sphere, an ellipsoid, a datum and a geoid.
- Define the semi-major axis, the semi-minor axis and the inverse flattening.
- Know how to distinguish between conical, cylindrical, and azimuthal projections.
- Recognize the standard Mercator projection.
- Define a great circle distance.
- Define Tissot’s indicatrix, and explain how is it used to observe projection distortions.
- Understand what it means to say that a projection is conformal, equidistance, equal-area (equivalent), or azimuthal.
- Understand the general characteristics of UTM and State Plane projections.
- Memorize the following EPSG projections’ names and their common uses (EPSG:4326, EPSG:3857, EPSG:2264, and EPSG:2163)
